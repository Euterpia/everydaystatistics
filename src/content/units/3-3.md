## Opening Hook

In July 2012, Fox Business broadcast a bar chart showing the impact of letting the Bush tax cuts expire. Two bars sat side by side. The left bar, labelled "Now," represented the current top income tax rate of 35 percent. The right bar, labelled "Jan 1, 2013," represented the rate if the cuts lapsed: 39.6 percent. The difference between those two numbers is 4.6 percentage points. A real change, worth illustrating.

Except the chart's vertical axis did not start at zero. It started at 34 percent. The visual result was that the right bar appeared to be roughly six times the height of the left bar. A viewer glancing at the screen, which is what most viewers do, would absorb the impression of a tax rate that had more than doubled, not one that had increased by less than five percentage points. The numbers were accurate. The picture was not.

This is the trick at the heart of visual manipulation. The numbers are usually right there on the chart. The manipulation happens in the gap between what the numbers say and what the picture shows. That gap can be engineered.

## The Concept

A graph is a translation. It takes numbers and converts them into a visual impression. The translation can be done honestly, or it can be done to serve a conclusion. What follows is the complete toolkit, the full range of ways that translation gets corrupted.

**The truncated y-axis** is the most common trick in the drawer and the one that worked so well for Fox in 2012. The y-axis (the vertical axis) normally starts at zero. When it starts somewhere above zero, any change in the data gets visually amplified. A line that rose from 96 to 99 looks, on a chart starting at 94, like it has roughly tripled. On a chart starting at zero, it barely moves.

This is not always wrong. Medical data where the clinically relevant range is 95 to 100 may legitimately use a truncated axis to show meaningful variation. The question is whether the axis choice is serving the data or serving a narrative. The tell is the axis label: check where zero is, and ask whether the visual impression you just had matches the actual magnitude of change.

**The inverted axis** is less common but more audacious. In 2014, a data visualisation created for Reuters showed Florida gun deaths by year, as a red area chart. The y-axis ran from zero at the top to roughly 900 at the bottom. Gun deaths rose sharply after Florida's Stand Your Ground law passed in 2005. On this chart, because the axis was inverted, the line fell sharply, toward the bottom of the chart, which most viewers read as a decline. The designer, Christine Chan, said she preferred to show deaths in "negative terms." The articles using the chart correctly stated that deaths had increased. But the chart itself showed the opposite. The visual impression and the truth were pointing in different directions.

**The dual axis** is the most flexible manipulation in the toolkit because it can be adjusted after the fact to show almost any relationship. A dual-axis chart puts two different datasets on the same graph, each with its own y-axis on either side. By changing the scale of either axis, the designer can make the two lines converge, cross, diverge, or mirror each other at will. Two completely unrelated trends can be made to look like they move in lockstep. Two trends that genuinely track each other can be made to look like they have nothing in common. The tell is the presence of two y-axes with different scales. When you see one, the correlation you are looking at is partly a product of axis choice, not just of the underlying data.

**The logarithmic scale** is genuinely useful and genuinely misunderstood. A logarithmic scale (log scale for short) is one where each step up the axis represents a multiplication rather than an addition. On a linear scale, going from 1 to 2 is the same visual distance as going from 100 to 101. On a log scale, going from 1 to 10 is the same distance as going from 10 to 100, and from 100 to 1,000. This is the honest use: log scales show rates of change and are appropriate when the data spans several orders of magnitude, as with earthquake magnitudes, or the early spread of a disease. During the Covid-19 pandemic, public health bodies used log scales to show case growth rates, which is legitimate. The misuse is publishing a log-scale chart to an audience unfamiliar with what it implies. Research published in 2020 found that fewer than half of participants shown a log-scale Covid graph could correctly answer a basic question about it, compared to over 80 percent shown a linear version. The same data, rendered in log scale for a general audience, produced lower estimates of the danger, lower support for public health measures, and lower stated intention to wear masks. The scale was honest. The communication was not.

**Area distortion** exploits a quirk of visual perception: when we see a shape, we estimate its size by area, not by height. A pictogram showing two oil barrels, one representing 2019 output and one representing 2020 output, that doubles the height of the smaller barrel to represent double the volume, is not doubling the area, it is quadrupling it. The visual impression is of a four-to-one difference when the actual difference is two-to-one. Three-dimensional pie charts do the same thing in a different direction: slices near the front of a tilted pie appear larger than slices at the back, even when they represent equal values. 3D charts almost never add information. They almost always distort perception.

**Missing uncertainty** is the trick of showing a line and implying it is precise when it is an estimate with a range around it. Every survey result, every economic forecast, every reported measurement has uncertainty attached to it. A graph showing a single line implies knowledge that, in most cases, is not there. When a polling graphic shows two candidates at 46 and 44 percent, without showing the margin of error, it creates the impression of a meaningful lead when the two numbers may be statistically indistinguishable. Presenting point estimates as certainties is a choice, and it is almost always the choice made in political communication and financial marketing.

**Chart type selection** is the subtlest manipulation because it operates before a single number appears. Different chart types create different default impressions. A pie chart, by its nature, draws the eye to the largest segment. A line chart implies continuity and trend. A bar chart implies comparison and ranking. These conventions can be exploited: plotting discrete, unrelated categories as a line graph implies a trend that does not exist; using a pie chart with many small segments creates an impression of dominance for the largest slice. The selection of chart type is a framing decision, and it is made before the reader sees the data.

## Why It Matters

The graph trick is not a minor aesthetic concern. It operates in domains where the stakes are high.

In political communication, it shapes how citizens assess the performance of governments. A governing party presenting economic data will choose the axis range, the start date, and the chart type that flatters the trend. An opposition party will make the opposite choices with the same numbers. Neither is technically lying. Both are engineering an impression.

In financial marketing, it determines how products are sold. Investment performance graphs frequently show returns over a time period chosen to start just before a strong run and end at a peak. Axis scales are compressed or expanded to make smooth returns look like they beat alternatives more decisively than they did. The Financial Conduct Authority in the UK has published guidance on misleading financial charts precisely because the pattern is so consistent.

In health journalism, the log scale problem documented during Covid was not unique to that period. Cancer survival statistics, drug efficacy claims, and risk comparisons are routinely shown in formats that create impressions the numbers do not support. A study published in the British Medical Journal found that graphs in pharmaceutical company publications significantly more often used truncated axes than graphs in independent journals covering the same data.

The Fox News examples are well-documented because Fox News is a major network whose graphics get screenshotted and analysed. But the pattern is structural, not partisan. The same set of tricks appear across news organisations, across political parties, across industries, and across countries. Once you can see the tricks, you see them everywhere.

A second Fox News case from 2014 shows a different variant. When Affordable Care Act sign-ups hit 6 million against a target of 7 million, Fox aired a bar chart comparing the two figures. The axis was unlabelled and appeared to start at approximately 6 million. The visual gap between the two bars looked dramatic. The actual gap was 1 million out of 7 million, roughly 14 percent. Fox corrected the chart the following day.

## How to Spot It

Three questions expose the majority of misleading graphs.

The first question is: where does the y-axis start? If it does not start at zero, ask what the impression would be if it did. The truncated axis is doing work. Whether that work is legitimate or manipulative depends on context and on whether the visual impression the truncated axis creates is proportionate to the actual magnitude of the change.

The second question is: how many axes are there? If there are two y-axes with different scales, the visual relationship between the two lines is partly a product of design choice. Do not accept the implied correlation. Check whether the two series actually move together or whether the alignment is an artefact of axis scaling.

The third question is: what is missing? Is the uncertainty shown? Is the baseline visible? Is the comparison period stated? Missing information is as consequential as present information. A graph that shows a trend from a carefully selected start date, without showing what came before, is editing the evidence.

For each specific trick, the tell is as follows. For the truncated axis: look at the axis label and calculate whether the visual impression is proportionate to the actual change. For the inverted axis: check whether "up" means more or less of the thing being measured, since it should almost always mean more. For the dual axis: look for two y-axes on either side of the chart; if present, treat any implied correlation with scepticism. For the log scale: look for the word "log" on the axis, or for tick marks that increase by multiplication (1, 10, 100, 1,000) rather than addition; ask whether the audience the chart is designed for understands what log scale means. For area distortion: when comparing two visual shapes, ask whether you are comparing height or area, and whether the designer has made the same distinction. For missing uncertainty: ask what the confidence interval or margin of error is, and whether showing it would change your reading of the chart. For chart type: ask whether a line connecting the data points implies a continuity that does not exist in the data.

## Your Challenge

A government department publishes a chart showing public satisfaction with a new healthcare policy. The chart is a line graph. It covers fourteen months, beginning two months after the policy launched. The line starts at 62 percent and rises to 71 percent. The y-axis begins at 60 percent. The chart has no confidence intervals shown. The x-axis label reads "Months since policy introduction."

What manipulations are present? How many are there? What would you need to see to form an honest assessment of whether public satisfaction has genuinely improved? What questions would you ask about the data behind the chart before taking it at face value?

There is no answer on this page. That is the point.
